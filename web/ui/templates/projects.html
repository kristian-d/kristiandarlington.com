Determining Presence of Vessel Transits in Spectrograms

This project, proposed by Sea to Shore Systems and the Saturna Island Marine Research & Education Society (SIMRES), tackles the problem of classifying spectrograms generated by undersea hydrophones as showing a marine vessel transit or not. The algorithm uses a combination of Gaussian smoothing and adaptive thresholding to prepare each spectogram, then determines the primary tones in the image using connected components labelling, connected components filtering, and frequency binning. This histogram of frequencies, along with a rough measure for the presence of Lloyd's Mirror Pattern, forms a feature vector that is supplied to a Support Vector Machine for final classification. Results were positive on the chosen dataset with approximately 86% values for all of accuracy, precision, recall, and F-score. The project is ongoing.

Queueing Systems Simulations Analysis

Statistical analysis on a variety of basic queueing systems is performed in this project; an M/M/1, M/G/1, M/M/c/N, and M/M/1 queue network are each equated to a scenario where airplanes are waiting in the airspace above a runway to land. In this analogy, the airspace acts as the queue and the runway acts as the service station. Simulations were run using the Java Modelling Tools software and results for server utilization, total number of customers in the system, total time spent in the system per customer, and waiting time per customer were compared with calculated theoretical values. In the case of the queues with Poisson arrival rates and exponential service times, theoretical values fell within confidence intervals in every case of every statistic. This was not the case for the M/G/1 queues, likely because theory for generalized service time distributions allows for greater error. Also included in the report are results of analysis on the JMT random number generator and its exponential distribution.

What Lyrics Have to Say

An interesting experiment in using classifiers of many varieties, "What Lyrics Have to Say" is a project completed with Becca Thomson, a UVIC Computer Science student, that determines the uniqueness of musical genres with respect to their lyrical content. Using a dataset of 380000 songs split into training and testing sets, the classifiers were trained on song vocabularies with their pre-classified genres, then tested by comparing the predicted genres of songs against the actual genres. Preparation of the data included the removal of non-English songs from the dataset and filtering of stop-words from each song. Results showed that Hip-Hop, Country, and Metal were the most identifiable genres by their typical lyrical content when Rock was not included. When Rock was included, however, all genres were significantly less identifiable; it is hypothesized that this is due to Rock being a versatile genre consisting of many different sounds and lyrical themes.

TreeGen: Determining the Optimal Planting Arrangement of Trees for Survival

TreeGen is an experiment in designing a genetic algorithm from start to finish and using it to find the optimal planting arrangements for the survival of trees given some environmental conditions. Conditions include the availability of water, availability of sunlight, and soil type in a particular area. The fitness function for the algorithm uses the ratio of surviving trees versus total competition for resources in the environment to provide a score; trees are deemed alive or dead by thresholding the amount of resources in the area surrounding the respective tree, while competition is calculated by summing the number of trees close to another tree. Crossovers and mutations were performed between generations as defined in the report. The algorithm terminates when the fitness score ceases to change over a defined number of generations. The project was completed alongside Rhett Devlin and Austin Lee, both UVIC Software Engineering students.
